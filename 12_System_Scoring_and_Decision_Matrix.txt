AURIC SENTINEL
DOCUMENT 12: SYSTEM SCORING AND DECISION MATRIX
================================================================

0. PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT

This document is the central decision intelligence reference for
AURIC SENTINEL. Where Document 04 (Signal Engine) and Document 11
(Trading Philosophy and Decision Framework) describe how the Signal
Engine itself works and why the system thinks the way it does, this
document exists as the consolidated master reference for exactly how
the system evaluates, ranks, approves, rejects, prioritizes, and
manages trading opportunities across every module, from first
candidate detection through to final trade resolution. This document
does not introduce new modules; it explains, in one unified place, how
every module described in Documents 01 through 11 contributes to the
system's final decision behaviour. This document is implementation-
independent throughout: it describes behaviour, logic, architecture,
and decision philosophy only, and contains no coding technique,
formula, algorithm, pseudocode, or variable of any kind.

================================================================
SECTION 1
SYSTEM DECISION PHILOSOPHY
================================================================

1.1 Overview

AURIC SENTINEL's decision engine exists to replace single-condition,
reactive trading decisions with a deliberate, multi-layer evaluation
process. The decision engine's core purpose is not to find as many
opportunities as possible, but to correctly identify the small subset
of market conditions where multiple, independent categories of
evidence genuinely agree with one another.

1.2 Why the System Rejects Most Trades

At any given moment, the majority of price behaviour on a chart is
noise, indecision, or partial/incomplete structural development. A
decision engine that acted on any single favorable-looking condition
would necessarily act on a large amount of this noise. AURIC SENTINEL
is deliberately built so that a candidate opportunity must survive
scrutiny from multiple independent evidence categories before it is
approved. Rejection is therefore not a shortcoming of the system; it is
the expected and correct outcome for the majority of candidate
conditions the system observes. A trader using this system should
understand that long periods without an approved signal reflect the
system functioning as designed, not underperforming.

1.3 Why Quality Is Always Preferred Over Quantity

The decision engine treats the cost of acting on a low-quality
opportunity as substantially higher than the cost of missing a
marginal opportunity. This is because a trader's ability to execute a
system consistently and confidently over time depends heavily on that
system maintaining a high signal-to-noise ratio. A system that
frequently produces weak or contradictory signals erodes the trader's
trust and encourages inconsistent execution, which undermines the
value of even the system's genuinely good signals. Quality-over-
quantity is therefore treated as a structural requirement of the
decision engine, not a tunable preference.

1.4 How Multiple Confirmation Layers Reduce False Signals

Each module in the system evaluates a distinct, independent category of
market evidence: directional trend behaviour, structural price action,
precise execution-timeframe triggers, momentum, and environmental
conditions such as session and volatility. Because these categories are
genuinely independent of one another rather than restatements of the
same underlying data, the probability that all of them align by
coincidence, rather than because a genuine high-quality opportunity
exists, is substantially lower than the probability of any single
category aligning by coincidence. This is the central statistical logic
behind why confirmation-layer agreement is treated as meaningfully more
reliable than any single indicator condition.

1.5 Institutional Thinking Compared With Retail Trading

Retail trading approaches frequently rely on a single indicator, a
single timeframe, or a single pattern in isolation, and often act
reactively to price movement that has already occurred (chasing).
Institutional-style thinking, which AURIC SENTINEL is built to emulate,
instead builds a layered thesis: establishing context first, requiring
structural confirmation of that context, waiting for a precise trigger
condition, and only then committing to a decision with pre-defined
risk. The decision engine's entire architecture exists to encode this
institutional sequence into a consistent, repeatable process, removing
the reactive, single-condition decision-making pattern that
characterizes less disciplined retail approaches.

SECTION 2
MODULE IMPORTANCE
================================================================

2.1 Overview

Every module in AURIC SENTINEL contributes a distinct category of
evidence to the final decision. This section describes each module's
conceptual role and relative responsibility within the decision engine,
without assigning any numeric weighting. Conceptual importance is
described qualitatively: foundational modules establish the conditions
under which later modules are even permitted to operate, while later
modules refine, filter, or act upon the foundation established earlier.

2.2 Trend Engine

The Trend Engine's responsibility is to establish the broad directional
thesis that all other modules operate within. Its importance is
foundational: without an established trend context, no other module has
a reliable frame of reference for what "aligned" or "conflicting"
evidence even means. A weak or absent trend context does not prevent
the system from operating, but it substantially raises the bar for
every subsequent module to independently justify a decision.

2.3 EMA Engine

The EMA Engine's responsibility is to characterize the behavioural
health and quality of the trend identified by the Trend Engine -
whether that trend is accelerating, decelerating, expanding,
compressing, or showing signs of exhaustion. Its importance is
refinement-level: it does not establish the thesis itself, but it
determines how much confidence the thesis deserves.

2.4 Market Structure

The Market Structure module's responsibility is to supply independent,
price-action-based structural evidence confirming or challenging the
directional thesis. Its importance is confirmatory and, in cases of
direct conflict with the trend, can be significant enough to override a
trend-based thesis, since structure often reflects more current
market behaviour than a lagging trend measure.

2.5 Price Action

The Price Action component's responsibility is to confirm that the
precise, granular behaviour of price at the moment of candidate
formation is consistent with a genuine trigger rather than incidental
movement. Its importance is precision-level: it does not establish
broad context, but it determines exact timing and genuine intent behind
a candidate setup.

2.6 Momentum

Momentum's responsibility is to confirm that the immediate rate and
quality of price movement supports the direction implied by trend,
structure, and price action. Its importance is corroborative: it adds
weight to an already-developing case rather than initiating one.

2.7 Filter Engine

The Filter Engine's responsibility is to ensure that even a technically
sound candidate opportunity is only acted upon under favorable
environmental conditions - appropriate session timing and appropriate
volatility. Its importance is gating: a technically excellent candidate
can still be correctly withheld from approval if conditions are
unfavorable, regardless of how strongly other modules agree.

2.8 Risk Management

Risk Management's responsibility is to ensure that any approved
decision carries complete, coherent risk framing before it is
considered actionable. Its importance is terminal-gating: even a
candidate that has passed every other module can still be correctly
rejected if it fails to produce a workable risk framework.

2.9 Dashboard

The Dashboard's responsibility is to make the complete state of every
other module's reasoning visible and understandable to the trader at a
glance. Its importance is communicative rather than decisional: it does
not influence the decision engine's conclusions, but it determines
whether the trader can actually trust and act on those conclusions
effectively.

2.10 Alerts

The Alerts module's responsibility is to extend the Dashboard's
communicative function beyond the moments the trader is actively
watching the chart. Its importance, like the Dashboard's, is
communicative rather than decisional.

2.11 Scanner

The Scanner's responsibility is to extend the complete decision engine
across multiple instruments simultaneously, without diluting the rigor
of that engine for any individual instrument. Its importance is
expansive: it multiplies the reach of the existing decision engine
rather than introducing new decision logic of its own.

SECTION 3
COMPLETE DECISION HIERARCHY
================================================================

3.1 Overview

The decision engine evaluates every candidate opportunity through a
strict, ordered sequence of stages. This section describes that
sequence, why the order is significant, how each stage depends on the
stages before it, and what happens when a stage is failed versus
passed.

3.2 The Order of Evaluation

The evaluation proceeds in the following conceptual order: broad
directional context, trend behavioural quality, structural
confirmation, precise price-action trigger, momentum confirmation,
environmental filtering, and finally risk validation, before a final
decision is reached. This mirrors the top-down reasoning process
described in Document 11, Section 6: broad, slower-moving evidence is
always evaluated before narrow, faster-moving evidence.

3.3 Why the Order Matters

Evaluating broad context before precise triggers ensures that the
system never commits to a decision based on a technically clean
execution-timeframe pattern that exists inside an unfavorable broader
context. If the order were reversed - triggers evaluated before context
- the system would risk treating incidental, context-less patterns as
equally valid to context-supported ones, undermining the entire
confluence-based philosophy described in Section 1.

3.4 Dependency of Every Stage

Each stage in the hierarchy depends on the presence of a usable
conclusion from the stage(s) before it. A stage that depends on an
earlier stage's output cannot proceed to a meaningful conclusion of its
own if that earlier output is itself undetermined or unavailable; in
such cases, the dependent stage must reflect that uncertainty rather
than substituting an assumed default.

3.5 What Happens if a Stage Fails

A stage failure carries one of two consequences, determined by the
nature of the failure: a hard failure (direct, confirmed conflict with
the candidate's direction) results in outright rejection of the
candidate, and evaluation stops immediately at that stage. A soft
failure (weak, incomplete, or ambiguous support, rather than direct
conflict) results in a confidence reduction being recorded, and
evaluation continues to the next stage, carrying that reduction
forward.

3.6 When Evaluation Stops

Evaluation stops immediately, without proceeding to any further stage,
whenever a hard failure occurs at any stage, or whenever an earlier
stage's output is entirely unavailable in a way that makes a later
stage's evaluation meaningless (for example, attempting to evaluate
structural confirmation when trend context itself is completely
undetermined).

3.7 When Evaluation Continues

Evaluation continues to the next stage whenever a stage is passed at
full standing, or whenever a stage results only in a soft failure
(confidence reduction) rather than a hard failure. The candidate
carries forward the complete accumulated record of every stage's
outcome, whether full standing, reduced confidence, or (at the final
stage) full rejection.

SECTION 4
SIGNAL CONFIDENCE PHILOSOPHY
================================================================

4.1 Overview

Every candidate that survives the decision hierarchy without a hard
rejection is assigned an overall confidence category, reflecting how
many stages were passed at full standing versus how many contributed a
confidence reduction, and how significant those reductions were.

4.2 Very High Confidence

Very High Confidence describes a candidate that passed every applicable
stage of the decision hierarchy at full standing, with no confidence
reductions recorded at any stage. This category represents the rare
condition where trend, structure, price action, momentum, and
environmental conditions are all in complete, independent agreement.
This category should be treated by the trader as the system's strongest
possible statement of conviction.

4.3 High Confidence

High Confidence describes a candidate that passed the majority of
stages at full standing, with only a single, relatively minor
confidence reduction recorded along the way. This category still
represents a strong, well-supported opportunity, but with slightly less
complete agreement than Very High Confidence.

4.4 Medium Confidence

Medium Confidence describes a candidate that accumulated multiple
confidence reductions, or one more significant reduction, while still
avoiding any hard rejection. This category represents a genuine,
still-valid opportunity, but one where the trader should apply
additional discretionary judgment given the less complete agreement
across evidence categories.

4.5 Low Confidence

Low Confidence describes a candidate that survived the hierarchy only
narrowly, having accumulated substantial confidence reductions across
several stages. This category exists primarily to support an optional
Aggressive Entry mode (Document 04, Section 22.17); under default and
Conservative configurations, this category should generally not be
presented as an actionable signal.

4.6 Rejected

Rejected describes any candidate that encountered a hard failure at any
stage of the hierarchy and was therefore stopped before reaching a
final decision. A rejected candidate is not assigned a confidence
category at all, since it never became an approved signal; it is
instead recorded with a specific rejection reason, per Document 04,
Section 22.12.

4.7 How Confidence Changes as Confirmations Increase or Decrease

Confidence is not fixed at the moment a candidate is first identified.
As a candidate progresses through the hierarchy, each additional stage
passed at full standing reinforces its confidence category, while each
additional soft failure erodes it. Furthermore, once a signal is
active, ongoing conditions can continue to affect its standing: renewed
structural confirmation or continued trend strength can be understood
as reinforcing the original confidence assessment, while emerging
weakness (such as Early Trend Weakness or Trend Exhaustion, per
Document 02, Section 22) should be understood as eroding it, even
though the original signal's approved status and risk framing remain
fixed once issued, consistent with the non-retroactive state-change
principle established in Document 08, Section 9.

SECTION 5
SIGNAL PRIORITY
================================================================

5.1 Overview

When multiple valid signals or candidates exist at once, whether on a
single instrument or across a watchlist, the system must be able to
express a clear priority ordering so the trader knows where to focus
attention first.

5.2 High Priority

High Priority describes an active signal in the Very High Confidence or
High Confidence category, occurring during a confirmed Trending market
phase, with no active environmental filter concerns. These signals
should be presented as deserving the trader's immediate primary
attention.

5.3 Medium Priority

Medium Priority describes an active signal in the Medium Confidence
category, or a High Confidence signal occurring under less favorable
environmental conditions (for example, a soft filter warning rather
than full approval). These signals deserve attention but with
somewhat more caution than High Priority signals.

5.4 Low Priority

Low Priority describes an active signal in the Low Confidence category,
typically only surfaced under an Aggressive Entry configuration. These
signals should be presented in a way that clearly communicates their
reduced standing relative to Medium and High Priority signals.

5.5 Institutional Grade

Institutional Grade describes the specific case of a High Probability
Entry as defined in Document 04, Section 22.19 - a candidate that not
only achieved Very High Confidence but also satisfied Multi-Timeframe
EMA Alignment and occurred during a fully confirmed Trending phase with
non-provisional structural confirmation. This grade represents the
system's absolute top-tier classification and should be visually and
categorically distinguished from all other grades.

5.6 Standard Grade

Standard Grade describes any approved signal that does not meet the
elevated combined criteria of Institutional Grade, but has otherwise
been fully approved through the decision hierarchy. This represents the
system's normal, expected output under typical favorable conditions.

5.7 Watchlist Grade

Watchlist Grade describes a candidate, evaluated through Scanner
Optimization (Document 09), that is progressing favorably through the
decision hierarchy on a non-primary watchlist symbol but has not yet
reached full approval. This grade exists to help the trader monitor
developing opportunities across a watchlist before they become fully
actionable.

5.8 Observation Only

Observation Only describes a candidate or condition that the system has
detected and is tracking, but which does not currently warrant any
elevated trader attention - for example, a pattern that failed a hard
requirement, or a market condition still in an early, undeveloped
phase. This grade exists primarily for the Debug/Full dashboard modes
and Scanner detail views rather than for direct trading action.

SECTION 6
CONFLICT RESOLUTION MATRIX
================================================================

6.1 Overview

This section describes how the decision engine must behave when
different modules produce disagreeing conclusions about the same
candidate opportunity. In every case, the general principle from
Section 3.5 applies: direct, confirmed conflicts produce hard
rejection, while partial or ambiguous disagreement produces a
confidence reduction rather than automatic rejection.

6.2 Trend Agrees, Structure Disagrees

When the Trend Engine favors a direction but the Market Structure
module reports a confirmed structural event against that direction,
this is treated as a significant conflict. If the structural conflict
is fully confirmed (not provisional), the candidate should be hard
rejected, since structure is treated as more immediately reflective of
current market behaviour than a lagging trend measure. If the
structural signal is still provisional or unconfirmed, the conflict
should instead produce a substantial confidence reduction rather than
outright rejection.

6.3 Trend Agrees, Momentum Disagrees

When the Trend Engine and Market Structure both favor a direction but
momentum fails to confirm it, this is treated as a caution flag rather
than a hard conflict, since momentum is inherently more volatile and
short-lived than trend or structure. This scenario should produce a
moderate confidence reduction, allowing the candidate to continue
through the hierarchy at a lower confidence tier rather than being
rejected outright.

6.4 EMA Agrees, Price Action Disagrees

When the EMA Engine reports healthy trend behaviour but the specific
execution-timeframe price action does not exhibit a clear, decisive
trigger pattern, this is treated as an incomplete case rather than a
conflict: the broader context remains favorable, but the precise
justification for acting now is missing. This scenario should prevent
the candidate from being approved at all in its current form, since the
Price Action stage functions as a required trigger rather than merely
a confidence contributor; the candidate should instead be held as an
Observation Only or Watchlist Grade item pending a clearer trigger.

6.5 Structure Agrees, Filter Rejects

When trend, structure, and trigger conditions all favor a candidate but
the Filter Engine rejects it due to unfavorable session or volatility
conditions, this is treated as a timing conflict rather than a
directional conflict. The candidate should be recorded as technically
valid but currently non-actionable, and should be re-evaluated
automatically if conditions later become favorable, rather than being
permanently discarded.

6.6 Trend Strong, Risk Unacceptable

When every directional and structural module strongly favors a
candidate, but the Risk Management module's safeguards determine that
no workable, reasonable risk framing can be established (for example,
due to an unreasonably tight or unreasonably wide stop-loss reference),
this is treated as a terminal conflict. The candidate should be
rejected regardless of how favorably every other module scored it,
consistent with the principle established in Document 04, Section
22.7, that a technically excellent setup with unworkable risk framing
provides no practical trading value.

SECTION 7
ENTRY CLASSIFICATION
================================================================

7.1 Overview

This section describes the distinct categories under which an approved
signal's entry may be classified, extending the confidence and priority
concepts described in Sections 4 and 5 into entry-specific terminology.

7.2 Institutional Entry

An Institutional Entry corresponds to the Institutional Grade
classification described in Section 5.5. It should exist only in the
rare cases where every layer of the system is in full, independent
agreement, and represents the system's highest possible statement of
conviction for entry timing.

7.3 High Probability Entry

High Probability Entry, as defined in Document 04, Section 22.19,
represents a top-tier but slightly broader category than Institutional
Entry, reserved for candidates passing every hierarchy stage at full
standing. This category should exist to give the trader a
clearly-distinguished tier above Standard Entry without requiring the
full combined criteria of an Institutional Entry.

7.4 Standard Entry

Standard Entry represents the system's normal, fully-approved output
under typical favorable conditions, corresponding to the Standard Grade
classification in Section 5.6. This category should exist as the
default, expected outcome of a well-functioning decision engine under
ordinary market conditions.

7.5 Aggressive Entry

Aggressive Entry, as defined in Document 04, Section 22.17, exists
specifically to allow candidates that accumulated confidence reductions
to still be presented as actionable, clearly lower-tier signals, when
the trader has explicitly opted into this reduced-selectivity mode.
This category should exist only when Aggressive Entry mode is active
and must always be visually distinguished from Standard and higher-tier
entries.

7.6 Conservative Entry

Conservative Entry, as defined in Document 04, Section 22.18, exists to
raise the approval bar beyond default settings, requiring full standing
at every applicable stage. This category should exist specifically for
traders who have opted into Conservative mode and prioritize maximum
selectivity over signal frequency.

7.7 Early Entry

Early Entry describes a condition where a trader (or, optionally, an
advanced system configuration) acts on a candidate before it has fully
completed the decision hierarchy - for example, acting on strong trend
and structure agreement before the precise price-action trigger has
fully confirmed. This category should exist only as an explicitly
labeled, higher-risk alternative to standard entries, never as the
system's default behaviour, since it inherently bypasses part of the
confirmation process described in Section 3.

7.8 Late Entry

Late Entry, as defined in Document 04, Section 22.15, describes acting
on a signal a meaningful period after its original issuance, once its
original risk framing may no longer be favorable. This category should
exist to help the trader recognize reduced suitability for fresh entry
on an aged signal, without erasing the signal's historical record.

SECTION 8
EXIT CLASSIFICATION
================================================================

8.1 Overview

This section describes the distinct categories under which an active
signal's resolution or exit may be classified, giving the trader clear
insight into why a trade concluded the way it did.

8.2 Normal Exit

A Normal Exit describes a signal reaching one of its predefined
take-profit reference levels (Document 08, Section 3) without any
earlier invalidation or protective event occurring. This represents the
system's baseline expected successful outcome.

8.3 Trend Exit

A Trend Exit describes a signal being closed or flagged for closure
because the underlying trend context that originally justified it has
meaningfully weakened or reversed (for example, confirmed Trend Failure
per Document 02, Section 22.15), even though price has not yet reached
either the original stop-loss or take-profit level. This exit type
exists to allow the trader to react to a changing thesis rather than
waiting rigidly for a fixed price level to be reached.

8.4 Structure Exit

A Structure Exit describes a signal being closed or flagged for closure
because a confirmed structural event (Document 03) directly
contradicts the original structural justification for the trade,
independent of trend condition. This exit type exists because
structural evidence can sometimes shift meaningfully before broader
trend classification does.

8.5 Momentum Exit

A Momentum Exit describes a signal being flagged for closure or
reduced confidence because momentum has decisively turned against the
trade's direction, even though trend and structure remain nominally
intact. This exit type exists to allow earlier reaction to weakening
conviction than waiting for a full trend or structure reversal.

8.6 Protective Exit

A Protective Exit describes the predefined breakeven or partial risk-
reduction mechanism (Document 08, Section 3) activating once a defined
milestone has been reached, shifting the trade's risk profile in the
trader's favor without necessarily closing the position entirely. This
exit type exists to formalize the system's capital-protection
philosophy described in Section 11.

8.7 Emergency Exit

An Emergency Exit describes a signal being flagged for immediate
closure consideration due to an extreme, abrupt market condition (for
example, extreme volatility or a severe gap event, per Section 12) that
falls outside the normal range of conditions the system's standard
exit logic was designed to handle gracefully. This exit type exists as
an acknowledgment that no rule-based system can fully anticipate every
extreme market condition, and that the trader should be alerted
distinctly when such a condition is detected.

8.8 Invalidation Exit

An Invalidation Exit describes a signal being closed because the
specific condition that originally justified it (Document 04, Section
22.13) has been directly and confirmedly violated - for example, price
decisively closing back through the structural zone that justified the
original entry. This exit type is the most direct and unambiguous of
the exit categories, since it reflects the system's own stated
justification no longer holding true.

SECTION 9
SIGNAL LIFECYCLE
================================================================

9.1 Overview

This section describes the complete set of states a signal may occupy
from initial detection through final resolution, and the allowed
transitions between them. This lifecycle extends and consolidates the
signal lifecycle concepts introduced in Document 04, Section 3, and the
trade/entry lifecycle concepts introduced in Document 11, Sections
14-16.

9.2 Candidate

Candidate describes a pattern that has begun to resemble a qualifying
setup but has not yet been evaluated through the complete decision
hierarchy. A Candidate may transition to Waiting, Confirmed, or be
silently discarded if it fails to develop further.

9.3 Waiting

Waiting describes a Candidate that has passed early hierarchy stages
(trend and structure context) but is still pending a precise price-
action trigger or momentum confirmation. Waiting may transition to
Confirmed, revert to Candidate if context conditions change, or be
discarded if the setup fails to develop.

9.4 Confirmed

Confirmed describes a candidate that has satisfied the price-action
trigger and momentum stages of the hierarchy and is now pending Filter
Engine and Risk Validation evaluation. Confirmed may transition to
Approved or to Rejected, per Section 3.5.

9.5 Approved

Approved describes a candidate that has passed every applicable stage
of the decision hierarchy, including Filter Engine and Risk Validation,
and has been assigned a confidence category and risk framing. Approved
transitions immediately to Active.

9.6 Active

Active describes an Approved signal currently being tracked for live
price movement toward its risk-defined outcomes. Active may transition
to Protected, Aging, Invalidated, or Completed.

9.7 Protected

Protected describes an Active signal that has reached its defined
breakeven or partial risk-reduction milestone (Section 8.6). Protected
signals remain a subtype of Active tracking and may still transition to
Aging, Invalidated, or Completed.

9.8 Aging

Aging describes an Active or Protected signal that has remained
unresolved beyond a defined threshold of time or price movement without
reaching either its stop or target (Document 04, Section 22.15). Aging
signals may still transition to Invalidated or Completed, but are
flagged distinctly to communicate reduced freshness.

9.9 Invalidated

Invalidated describes a signal closed due to any of the Trend Exit,
Structure Exit, Momentum Exit, or Invalidation Exit conditions
described in Section 8, prior to reaching its original stop or target.
Invalidated is a terminal state.

9.10 Expired

Expired describes a Candidate, Waiting, or Confirmed state that failed
to progress to Approved within its natural evaluation window and is
therefore discarded before ever becoming an active trade idea. Expired
is a terminal state for pre-approval stages only.

9.11 Completed

Completed describes an Active or Protected signal that reached one of
its predefined take-profit levels or its stop-loss level (a Normal Exit
or Protective stop outcome). Completed is a terminal state.

9.12 Cancelled

Cancelled describes any signal state manually or systemically withdrawn
due to an Emergency Exit condition (Section 8.7) or a fundamental data/
system integrity concern (for example, a degraded upstream module
state, per Document 01, Section 10) rather than a normal market-driven
resolution. Cancelled is a terminal state.

SECTION 10
SYSTEM STATE DEFINITIONS
================================================================

10.1 Overview

In addition to individual signal lifecycle states (Section 9), the
overall AURIC SENTINEL system itself occupies one of several
operating states at any given time, describing its general posture
rather than any single signal's condition.

10.2 Idle

Idle describes the system state where no candidate, waiting, or active
signal currently exists on the primary instrument, and no significant
developing condition has been detected. The system continues normal
evaluation but has nothing elevated to report.

10.3 Scanning

Scanning describes the system state, applicable when Scanner
Optimization (Document 09) is enabled, in which the system is actively
evaluating the broader watchlist for developing conditions, independent
of the primary instrument's own current state.

10.4 Waiting

Waiting, at the system level, describes the state where a Candidate or
Waiting-stage signal (Section 9.2-9.3) exists on the primary instrument
and the system is actively monitoring its development toward
confirmation.

10.5 Signal Building

Signal Building describes the state where a Confirmed-stage signal
(Section 9.4) is pending final Filter Engine and Risk Validation
evaluation, representing the final moments before an approval or
rejection decision is reached.

10.6 Signal Confirmed

Signal Confirmed, at the system level, describes the moment a candidate
has just transitioned to Approved (Section 9.5), representing the
system's active communication point for Dashboard emphasis and Alerts
firing.

10.7 Trade Active

Trade Active describes the state where an Active or Protected signal
(Sections 9.6-9.7) currently exists on the primary instrument, and the
system's primary function is monitoring that signal toward resolution.

10.8 Cooldown

Cooldown describes the state immediately following a signal's
resolution (Completed, Invalidated, or Cancelled) during which the
system's signal memory (Document 04, Section 3) prevents an
essentially identical new candidate from immediately re-triggering,
consistent with the duplicate-prevention requirement established
there.

10.9 Recovery

Recovery describes the state following a Cancelled signal (Section
9.12) or a detected system/data integrity concern, during which the
system re-establishes reliable upstream module data before resuming
normal candidate evaluation, avoiding premature re-engagement based on
potentially unreliable data.

10.10 High Volatility

High Volatility describes a system-level state, driven by the Filter
Engine's volatility evaluation (Document 05), in which current
conditions exceed the instrument's normal volatility range. This state
should be clearly communicated system-wide, since it affects Filter
Engine approval behaviour, Risk Management's safeguard thresholds
(Document 08, Section 12), and may itself be a precondition for
Emergency Exit consideration (Section 8.7).

10.11 Low Liquidity

Low Liquidity describes a system-level state corresponding to session
or instrument conditions where reliable price discovery is less certain
than normal (for example, outside approved session windows, per
Document 05). This state should generally coincide with the Filter
Engine withholding signal approval.

10.12 Maintenance Mode

Maintenance Mode describes a state in which the trader has manually
disabled one or more core modules, or the system has detected a
sustained upstream data integrity concern (Document 01, Section 10)
significant enough that normal candidate evaluation should not proceed
with full confidence. The Dashboard must clearly and persistently
indicate Maintenance Mode whenever active.

SECTION 11
PROFESSIONAL TRADING PRINCIPLES
================================================================

11.1 Never Chase Price

The decision engine must never approve a candidate solely because price
has already moved significantly in a favorable direction. Every
approval must be justified by the complete decision hierarchy
described in Section 3, not by the appeal of recent favorable price
movement alone.

11.2 Wait for Confirmation

The decision engine must always require the applicable confirmation
stages described in Section 3 to be satisfied before approval,
resisting any design pressure to approve candidates earlier in order to
increase signal frequency.

11.3 Protect Capital First

Risk Validation (Section 2.8, Section 6.6) must always be treated as a
non-negotiable gating stage. No candidate, regardless of how favorably
every other module scored it, may be approved without a workable,
reasonable risk framework already attached.

11.4 Reject Weak Setups

Consistent with Section 1.2, the decision engine must treat rejection
as a normal, healthy, and frequent outcome rather than a failure mode
to be minimized through loosened standards.

11.5 One Excellent Trade Is Better Than Many Average Trades

The decision engine's tiered confidence and priority system (Sections
4 and 5) exists specifically to help the trader recognize and
prioritize the rare Institutional Grade or High Probability
opportunities over a larger volume of Standard or lower-tier signals,
reinforcing that signal quality distribution matters more than signal
count.

11.6 Patience Creates Consistency

The Aging and Cooldown states (Sections 9.8 and 10.8) exist specifically
to prevent the system from encouraging impatient, repeated action on
stale or recently-resolved conditions, reinforcing a patient,
deliberate trading cadence.

11.7 Probability Is More Important Than Prediction

The decision engine never claims certainty about any individual
outcome; every confidence category (Section 4) and priority tier
(Section 5) is a statement about the relative strength of current
evidence, not a prediction of a guaranteed result. This principle must
be reflected in all trader-facing language across the Dashboard and
Alerts modules, consistent with the risk-framing language requirement
established in Document 08, Section 4.

SECTION 12
EDGE CASE DECISION RULES
================================================================

12.1 Conflicting Confirmations

When two or more modules produce genuinely ambiguous, neither-clearly-
agreeing-nor-clearly-conflicting output at the same time, the decision
engine must default toward caution: treat the ambiguity as a confidence
reduction rather than either a hard rejection or a full-standing pass,
consistent with the general soft-failure handling described in Section
3.5.

12.2 Fast Reversals

When price reverses sharply and rapidly against an Active signal
without a clean, orderly structural or trend transition, this should be
treated as a candidate Emergency Exit condition (Section 8.7) pending
confirmation, since rapid, disorderly reversals often fall outside the
normal, gradual invalidation patterns the standard exit classifications
were designed around.

12.3 Gap Openings

When an instrument's price opens with a significant gap relative to its
prior close, any Active signal's risk framing should be re-evaluated
for continued validity (since original stop/target distances may no
longer reflect a reasonable risk profile), and any Candidate or Waiting
stage evaluation occurring immediately after a significant gap should
be treated with elevated caution given the reduced reliability of
immediate post-gap price behaviour.

12.4 Extreme Volatility

Extreme Volatility, a more severe condition than the standard High
Volatility system state (Section 10.10), should trigger consideration
of Emergency Exit for any Active signal and should prevent new
candidate approval entirely until conditions normalize, since normal
risk framing assumptions (Document 08) are least reliable under these
conditions.

12.5 Low Liquidity

Beyond the standard Low Liquidity system state handling (Section
10.11), sustained low liquidity affecting an already-Active signal
should be flagged distinctly on the Dashboard, since the reliability of
that signal's ongoing price tracking is reduced even though the signal
itself remains open.

12.6 Session Transitions

Candidate or Waiting-stage evaluation occurring in the narrow window
immediately surrounding a defined session boundary transition should be
treated with the same boundary-condition care described in Document 05,
Section 12, applying a clearly defined inclusive/exclusive rule rather
than ambiguous behaviour.

12.7 News Spikes

A sudden, sharp price movement inconsistent with the gradual behaviour
patterns the system's momentum and structure logic were designed
around should be treated as a distinct, flagged condition, since it may
reflect a scheduled or unscheduled news event rather than genuine
technical development. Any Candidate developing during such a spike
should be held at a reduced confidence tier pending confirmation that
the movement reflects genuine, sustained conditions rather than a
temporary spike.

12.8 False Breakouts

A structural event (Document 03) that initially appears to confirm but
is quickly and decisively reversed should be retrospectively reflected
as a Structure Failure condition (Document 03, Section 22.11) rather
than a valid confirmed structural event, and any candidate that was
approved based on the now-reversed structural event should be
transitioned toward Invalidated (Section 9.9) rather than remaining
Active without re-evaluation.

12.9 Trend Exhaustion

A candidate developing during a confirmed Trend Exhaustion state
(Document 02, Section 22.13) should be held to a stricter confidence
standard than normal, since the broader trend context underlying the
candidate is already flagged as elevated-risk for reversal.

12.10 Multiple Simultaneous Signals

When more than one candidate reaches Approved status at effectively the
same time (whether on the same instrument at different structural
levels, or across a watchlist via Scanner Optimization), the Signal
Priority framework (Section 5) must be applied to clearly communicate
which deserves primary attention, rather than presenting all
simultaneous signals as equally weighted.

SECTION 13
QUALITY ASSURANCE
================================================================

13.1 Overview

This section defines the acceptance standard that the complete decision
engine, as described throughout this document, must meet.

13.2 Traceable

Every decision - approval, rejection, confidence category, priority
tier, or exit classification - must be traceable to the specific
stage(s) and specific module output(s) that produced it, consistent
with the traceability principle established throughout Documents 04,
05, and 11.

13.3 Explainable

Every decision must be describable to the trader in plain language
without requiring inspection of internal module workings, consistent
with the Dashboard's communicative role described in Section 2.9.

13.4 Repeatable

Given the same historical data, the same decision must be produced
every time the system evaluates it, consistent with the determinism
requirement established in Document 04, Section 4.

13.5 Deterministic

No decision may depend on randomness, session-specific variation, or
any factor other than the defined evidence categories described
throughout this document and Documents 01 through 11.

13.6 Consistent

The same category of evidence must be treated the same way whenever it
occurs, across different instruments, sessions, and time periods,
consistent with the consistency philosophy established in Document 11,
Section 18.

13.7 Non-Repainting

No decision, once displayed to the trader, may be retroactively altered
on historical review, consistent with the non-repainting requirement
established throughout every document in this set.

13.8 Professional

Every decision and every piece of trader-facing communication about
that decision must reflect the disciplined, probability-based
professional principles described in Section 11, never overstating
certainty or encouraging impulsive action.

13.9 Institutional

The overall character of the decision engine, taken as a whole, must
reflect the institutional thinking described in Section 1.5: layered,
patient, evidence-based, and risk-first, rather than reactive or
single-condition-driven.

SECTION 14
FUTURE SCALABILITY
================================================================

14.1 Overview

This section describes how future additional modules or capabilities
can be integrated into the decision engine described in this document
without altering its core philosophy or decision sequence, consistent
with the future scalability philosophy established in Document 11,
Section 20.

14.2 Smart Money Concepts, Order Blocks, Fair Value Gaps, Liquidity
Sweeps

Any future expansion of institutional/smart-money concepts beyond what
is already described in Document 03 must integrate as additional
structural evidence within the existing Market Structure module's
conceptual role (Section 2.4), contributing to the same structural
confirmation stage of the hierarchy (Section 3.2) rather than
introducing a separate, parallel decision path.

14.3 Volume Profile

A future volume-based analysis capability must integrate as an
additional environmental or confirmatory evidence category, most
naturally alongside the Filter Engine's or Momentum's conceptual role
(Sections 2.6-2.7), without altering the fundamental order-of-evaluation
principle described in Section 3.3.

14.4 RSI Engine

A future momentum-oscillator-based capability must integrate within the
existing Momentum stage of the hierarchy (Section 3.2, Section 2.6),
contributing additional corroborative evidence rather than replacing
the existing momentum evaluation concept.

14.5 Machine Learning Confidence

A future machine-learning-derived confidence contribution, if ever
introduced, must be integrated as an additional, clearly-labeled input
to the existing Signal Confidence framework (Section 4), and must
remain subject to the same Traceable, Explainable, and Deterministic
requirements described in Section 13; a black-box contribution that
cannot be explained to the trader would violate the core quality
assurance standard of this document and must not be integrated in a
way that bypasses that standard.

14.6 AI Trade Assistant

A future conversational or assistant-based interface layered on top of
AURIC SENTINEL must draw exclusively from the same finalized decision
engine output described in this document, functioning as an additional
communication layer analogous to the Dashboard and Alerts modules
(Sections 2.9-2.10), never as an independent source of trading
conclusions.

14.7 Multi-Asset Scanner

Any future expansion of the Scanner module's capability (Document 09)
beyond a single instrument class must continue to apply the complete,
unaltered decision hierarchy described in Section 3 to every additional
asset class, consistent with the core reuse principle already
established in Document 09, Section 22.11, ensuring rigor is never
diluted by broader reach.

14.8 Future Dashboard Upgrades

Any future Dashboard capability must continue to adhere strictly to the
presentation-only responsibility described in Section 2.9 and Document
06, Section 15: new visual capabilities may present decision engine
output in new ways, but must never introduce new decision-making logic
of their own.
